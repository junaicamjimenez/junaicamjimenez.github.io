Water Fasting 101: What You Need to Know


                                               



What is Water Fasting Exactly?

There may be no easier diet to define than water fasting, for this approach involves exactly what it says. “Water fasting is complete absence of all substances, except pure water, in an environment of complete rest,” says Alan Goldhamer, doctor of chiropractic and founder of the TrueNorth Health Center in Santa Rosa, California, who runs medically supervised water fasts that last anywhere from 5 to 40 days (the latter of which is a length of time most healthcare professionals do not endorse). The idea behind being at complete rest (aka not exercising), Dr. Goldhamer says, is that your body won’t need as many calories.


Health Benefits of Water Fasting

Others, of course, can fast for religious purposes or to prepare for a medical procedure such as a colonoscopy.

In the holistic wellness community, water fasts are a hot subject, but let's get one thing straight: despite what you might have seen elsewhere, there isn't enough rigorous clinical research to justify using this method for weight loss, diabetes control, and the like. In reality, for certain people, water fasts can be dangerous to their health.

Taking a rest from food, on the other hand, may help to reduce blood pressure. In a previous study, 174 people with elevated blood pressure fasted for 10 to 11 days on average, and by the end of the time, almost 90% of the participants' blood pressure had dropped to normal levels.


Who Should Avoid Doing a Water Fast and Why?

They can be avoided by some people because they can cause severe health problems. Many with renal failure, those taking various prescriptions, and women who are pregnant or breastfeeding are among those who are at risk, according to Taz. She also advises against doing this on your own whether you have diabetes, heart disease, or liver disease. Anyone with a history of eating disorders should avoid this method.
